Introduction

Vitreoscilla hemoglobin (VHb) is a protein found in the aerobic gram negative bacteria Vitreoscilla spp. It is regulated by the vitreoscilla hemoglobin promoter which is oxygen dependent and activates the transcription of the hemoglobin under microaerobic conditions to increase oxygen uptake [1]. It was first discovered in 1966 and was initially regarded a cytochrome until the disclosure of its primary structure in 1986 of which it was immediately recognized as a hemoglobin [3]. Shortly after the gene (vgb) was for the first time cloned and expressed recombinantly in Escherichia coli (E. coli) in 1988, its capability to increase oxygen uptake was immediately noted [4][5].

The discovery started a new approach in combating the reduced yields in fermentors caused by low oxygen conditions arising from high density cell cultures. Instead of modifying environmental parameters in order to increase oxygen availability, hemoglobin is instead co-expressed in order to enhance the cellular oxygen utilization. In this way, the cells are able to aerobically catabolize the available carbon sources under microaerobic conditions without generating growth-inhibitory metabolites such as lactate and acetate which is the major bottleneck under these sparse conditions [5]. With this, it has been found that in addition of being able to increase protein yields under microaerobic conditions, the presence is also correlated with improved cell growth and survivability [5]. This seems to be the case for not only Vitreoscilla and E. coli, but also for other bacterial species [6], yeast [8], plants[9] and even some vertebrates [10].

Structure

The protein is a homodimeric hemoglobin, consisting of two 146 amino acid residue subunits with a close resemblance to the globin fold [2]. The globin fold is a three dimensional motif shared among the globin superfamily and consists of six to eight alpha-helices packed in an approximate 50 degree angle. Starting from the N-terminus, each helix is denoted as helix A-H. Between each helix there is a loop connecting the segments, denoted by AB, BC, CD etc., corresponding to each pair of helices. The pocket at which heme is located is formed by a hydrophobic cavity created by the distal E-helix and proximal F-helix by which the conserved proximal histidine F8 coordinates with the upper portion of the heme-group.

The VHb monomer is illustrated fig. 2 along with the sperm whale myoglobin (Mb) which is commonly considered as the reference monomeric hemoglobin. The globin fold is clearly visible and the similarities are apparent with the heme residing in the pocket enclosed by the E- and F alpha helices. However, while the globin fold of Mb consists of eight alpha helices, the VHb has only seven as there is no apparent structure in the expected D-helix region [19]. This gives rise to a hydrophobic CE transition in contrast to the usual CD, which along with the initial hydrophobic and disordered E7-E10 segment of the E-helix is believed to one of the key reasons in the high lipid binding propensity of VHb [11][19].

Role in oxygen delivery

It seems that the way in which VHb enhances oxygen uptake is by acting as an oxygen transporter by directly binding to oxygen and delivering it to the respiratory chain. This claim is supported by several studies covering different mechanisms of VHb. More specifically, there are two key observations that motivate this.

First, it has been shown that VHb has a high in vitro binding affinity to lipid monolayers extracted from E. coli and that once bound, the oxygen affinity is reduced by more than 20-fold [11]. This is believed to be caused by the hydrophobic region between the CE-corner and the E-helix at the distal pocket serving as an anchor to the acyl chains of the phospholipid bilayer. By the consequent interaction, a conformational change would occur around the heme pocket and thereby immediately reduce the oxygen binding affinity.

Secondly, it has been shown by immunogold labeling in electron microscopy that VHb is localized to the cytoplasm and to a large extent by the cell membrane in both Vitreoscilla and E. coli [12]. These two observations indicate that VHb indeed transports oxygen to the respiratory chain, since if VHb is not in proximity to the cell membrane, it has high affinity for oxygen and vice versa, if bound the affinity is greatly reduced. Now, since respiration occurs around cell membranes, it seems highly plausible that VHb transports oxygen to the respiratory chain via direct interaction.

Other supporting experiments includes showing that VHb can stimulate oxygenase activity [13][14] and even itself serve as a terminal oxidase [15]. The latter was shown by letting cells without any terminal oxidases cultivate under aerobic conditions, of which only cells containing VHb could maintain an aerobic respiration. Moreover, it has been shown that VHb protects from oxidative stress [16] [17] and that increased expression levels are correlated with improved cell growth [18].
